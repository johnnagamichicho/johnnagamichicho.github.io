November 12, 1998

To a Haiku Writer, Spam Is Poetry in a Can
By MATTHEW MIRAPAUL
or the moment, anyway, John Nagamichi Cho's new book has a category unto itself at the Library of Congress. His Internet-derived collection of haiku-like verse inspired by Spam, the popular processed-pork product, is the sole listing under the subject heading "Canned meat -- poetry."

With the deaths last year of the Beat poets Allen Ginsberg and William S. Burroughs, Cho crowed, "I'm the first in a generation of 'meat poets.' "

For "Spam-ku: Tranquil Reflections on Luncheon Loaf" (88 pp., HarperCollins, $7.95), Cho sliced 162 prime examples of porcine poetry out of the Spam Haiku Archive, a Web site he launched as a lark in June 1995. To date, about 1,500 online poets have submitted more than 11,000 entries to the site. All refer in some manner to the rosy-toned Hormel Foods product, and most follow the three-line, 5-7-5 pattern of syllables that schoolchildren learn is the classic haiku form. The Spam-ku range from earnest, often ham-fisted efforts at evoking a mood, such as Cho's "The first time we kissed:/ Your cheeks flushed a deep Spam pink,/ Your lips soft, hungry" to such swinishly punny entries as Chris Fishel's "Descartes on pig parts/ Says: I'm pink, therefore I'm Spam/ Deep philosophy."
'It's technically not poetry. Spam haiku makes statements; statements are not the same as images.'

Cho, a 34-year-old research scientist at M.I.T., attributes part of Spam-ku's popularity to the simple fact that it is a short, silly-sounding word. "It's hard to compose haiku about Vienna sausage or Canadian bacon," Cho said. "They take up too many syllables.

He said that contributors liked to play with the tension between the nostalgia invoked by Spam's rich history -- introduced in 1937, it was a staple of many wartime diets -- and its anachronistic status today as processed pork in a fresh-meat era.

More than anything, though, Spam-ku are easy to compose, an appealing ingredient for those taking advantage of the Web's self-publishing capabilities. Cho's site also contains an area for haiku about news events, and earlier this year Salon, the online magazine, held a competition for haiku error messages.

"American poetry in the 20th Century has become pretty intimidating," Cho said. "Both rhyme and meter have gone out of fashion, and people find it hard to understand what poetry is and how to write it. Here, you have a very simple structure that anyone can follow -- although you'd be surprised how many entries I get that miss the syllable count."

Francis Heaney has written about 230 Spam-ku for the archive. "They're not all gems," Heaney conceded. His favorite -- "Moon rises,/ White as two slices of bread,/ Sunset pink as Spam within" -- did not make it into Cho's book.

Although he has since abandoned the form, Heaney, a 28-year-old puzzle writer and editor in Manhattan, said, "I do my best writing under constraints, so the constraint of a haiku, plus the fact that it has to be about Spam, is very limiting. To do things within that is fun."
Descartes on pig parts Says: I'm pink, therefore I'm Spam Deep philosophy.

Because haiku almost always involves nature, Heaney sought to incorporate that element into his poems.

Would that requirement be fulfilled by a reference to Spam alone? "It's made out of pigs," Heaney replied. "I guess pigs are natural."

Another entry that did not make it into the book was this candidate from Alex Dunne: "Spam's own irony/ Is that its haiku's never/ Worse than Spam itself."

Haiku experts are skeptical about the new form. "It's technically not poetry," said Lee Gurga, a dentist from Lincoln, Ill., who recently served as president of the Haiku Society of America. "Spam haiku makes statements; statements are not the same as images."

Despite Cho's claims to the contrary, Dr. Gurga said, the archive entries are not "senryu" either. "Senryu have to do with human nature, and they can be very funny, but they tend to have a little more resonance than Spam." Ultimately, Dr. Gurga said, Spam-ku "doesn't bother me in the least," adding, "I like it when people have fun."

And Dr. Gurga said he hoped that the archive would lead visitors to more serious haiku sites on the Internet. Well-regarded examples include Dhugal J. Lindsay's Haiku Universe and the Shiki Internet Haiku Salon.

Cho's slim volume represents a burgeoning breed of books that were sired in cyberspace. Jeremie Ruby-Strauss, who edited "Spam-ku" for HarperCollins and has published a compendium of martini recipes siphoned from a Web site, approached Cho after stumbling upon his archive.

Ruby-Strauss, who uses an unopened can of Spam as a paperweight, said he had seen Spam-ku swapped with gusto elsewhere on the Net.

"Here's a product that's been marketed," Ruby-Strauss said of Spam, but there was no literary product. "So let's make a product."

At his publisher's insistence, Cho sent his selections to Hormel at its headquarters in Austin, Minn., for approval. He omitted those he feared might raise objections.
Moon rises, White as two slices of bread, Sunset pink as Spam within.

For the most part, Cho said, "They just wanted all the capitalizations right and for some strange reason, they said no possessives."

The book's illustrations received the toughest inspection. One that showed a mouse sniffing at a tin of Spam was rejected by the company.

"They said, 'Please, no rodents associated with Spam,' " Cho recalled. "We just decided to can that one."

Hormel officials declined to comment, but the company does maintain a tongue-in-cheek Web site for its product (www.spam.com).

In addition to offering a Spam mouse pad for sale, the site contains a dissertation that strives to distance the company from "spam," Internet slang for unsolicited e-mail. The term was derived from a Monty Python comedy routine in which the word is repeated ad nauseum.

A few weeks ago, at the bidding of a friend's daughter who enjoyed the book and wanted to sample its salty, spicy subject, Cho consumed Spam for the first time in nearly 20 years. Despite his seven-syllable description of the sensation -- an "elastic, rubbery feel" -- he said the experience had not prompted him to compose a haiku about the moment. After penning 200 entries for the online archive, Cho lost his appetite for the verse form in early 1996.

"It was like an addiction," he recalled. "When it was over, it was completely over. I went cold turkey."
