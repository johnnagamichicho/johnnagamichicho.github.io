Spam's Back, But Leave Me Out

ome foods command respect: The T-bone steak. A slab of ribs. Tabasco. Coffee.

And then there's Spam.

Spam: That shimmering slab of pork . . . uh . . . parts, invented by Jay C. Hormel 62 years ago. Equally revered and reviled by the generation of G.I.s who ate it — often cold from the can — in World War II, Spam was congealing into low-key middle age.

That is until somehow, inexplicably, it won the hipness lottery.

Think Tony Bennett, but pinker.

Suddenly, Spam is hotter than a pig in a microwave. You've got your Spam T-shirts, hats, boxers, earrings (they look like little cans), even Spam mouse pads. For nostalgia lovers, there's the sterling silver Spam key necklace.

And now comes "SPAM-ku" (Harper Perennial, $7.95), a book of Spam haiku culled from more than 10,000 entries to the Internet.

Perfection uncanned
Like a beautiful redhead
Fresh from her trailer.

***

The beach picnic ends
The sand blows, the naked Spam
Puts on a tweed coat.

***

Made a Spam puppet
To entertain my doggie.
Need a new hand now.

As poetic inspiration, Spam is giving rosy-fingered dawn a run for her money.

But since my father will actually leave the room when anyone mentions the stuff (yes, he's a vet), it is difficult to understand just why this by-product product has become the object of such veneration — not to mention 77 cook-offs and a museum that attracts 20,000 visitors a month.

"If we could figure it out," said Hormel spokeswoman Meri Harris, "we'd do it with all our other products."

In fact, there are other products experiencing an equally surreal renaissance . . . just not at Hormel. (Certainly not their Dinty Moore canned stews, for instance.)

Midi-dresses, Burt Bacharach and cigars are all basking in new popularity. So is the Volkswagen Beetle. Heck, so is Clinton.

Even so, none of them is inspiring the Elvis-like idolization of Spam, which now has its own fan club, newsletter and secret handshake. (You might want to bring along a napkin.)

Clearly, Spam is exuding a truth so powerful (are we not all slabs of spiced ham, stuffed into the deep blue can of life?) it can only be captured in poetry.

"Am I Spam?" we ask ourselves, and conclude, "Spam I am."

Which is why our lives can be boiled down, like the poor Hormel pigs, to more SPAM-ku:

The first time we kissed,
Your cheeks flushed a deep Spam pink,
Your lips soft, hungry.

***

Four children later,
We run out of things to say.
"Price of Spam is up."

***

"Breakfast in bed, love."
"What is it, dear?" "Refried Spam."
You filed for divorce.
