The poet's inspiration: Spam

By Globe Staff, 10/23/98

IT researcher John Nagamichi Cho did not invent Spam haiku. Imagistic, three-line poems about the humble processed meat were already floating around the Internet when he put up a Web page to collect them.

But his new book, ''Spam-ku: Tranquil Reflections on Luncheon Loaf,'' is without question the definitive text on the form.

''I've tried to analyze it - why Spam and why haiku?'' said Cho, whose on-line collection has now grown to more than 11,000 entries. ''It was kind of like the `Field of Dreams.' I put up the archive, and Spam haiku flocked to the site.''

Most of the odes to what Cho calls ''the enigmatic porcine muse'' are actually ''senryu'' rather than haiku, which is a stricter poetic form, he says. The book's entries embrace a range of categories, including nature and romance. It leaves out two of the more popular themes: childhood trauma and cannibalism.

''The publisher insisted on clearing it with Hormel,'' Cho said. ''So there were things we weren't able to put in the book.''

A researcher of atmospheric sciences, Cho has some ideas as to why Spam-ku is so popular: Spam itself is ambiguous. It's monosyllabic. Best of all, it's ironic.

''Spam was really a genuine hero for World War II. It helped feed our soldiers,'' he said. ''But these days it's an anachronism. Who needs canned meat? The nostalgia and ridicule are a nice ironic tension.''

There's another irresistible aspect, too, he said.

''The word itself. Spam. It doesn't sound like food,'' he said. ''It sounds funny.''

Black cat drags about

his pink, purloined bite of SPAM like an extra tongue.

S even cans of SPAM stuffed inside my evening bag

Now I feel secure.

Four children later

We run out of things to say ''Price of SPAM is up.''

Bright lights and cruel cops grill the suspect pork product,

It doesn't confess.

I sing little songs

To help the SPAM stay cheerful Until I fry it.

I put my shoes on

But remembered far too late my secret SPAM stash.
